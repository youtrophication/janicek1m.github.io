Abstract

Sub-Saharan Africa is confronted by a demographic structure that will see a large part of the population come of working age over the next few years. Providing decent work, especially to the youth, will be a challenge and governments are looking for solutions to tackle the problem. There is increasing evidence that startup incubators boost employment creation. While the early stage incubation programs appear to be effective, there is still a gap when it comes to scaling and deal flow. This paper argues incubators are a potential tool for employment creation but more importantly they also empower participants. For countries with a high youth unemployment rate, providing a positive outlook on the future can be a core argument in favour of incubators.

Introduction

Developing countries in general, and those on the African continent in particular, are faced with a demographic structure where large numbers of the population will be of working age in the coming years and where there are few employment opportunities for younger generations entering the work force. According to the International Labour Organization (ILO), most of the Sub-Saharan African region is at risk, with more than 1 million individuals becoming unemployed in 2017; 72% of whom are working in vulnerable conditions.

The lack of employment generally has the potential to cause social unrest, while the quality of existing jobs is a contributing factor to social discontent as work is an important trademark of human identity in today’s world. The population structure however, can also provide for a significant demographic dividend. The term demographic dividend signifies a reduction of the youth dependency ratio in this context and results in positive effects in terms of “labour supply, savings and human capital”.

Sustainable development goal (SDG) 8 aims to promote “sustained, inclusive and sustainable economic growth, full and productive employment and decent work for all”. As incubators are one potential pathway for job creation, the focus of this paper links to the topic of “Youth Entrepreneurship & Self-Employment” as provided in the publication concept note.

Methods

This short paper is based on a literature review and connected to a report produced for the Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation (SDC) during a Master’s class entitled “Practical Project in Development Cooperation” at the University of St. Gallen. The class required students to consult for the SDC and granted access to experts from the field to have in-depth discussions with them about the topic of this short paper.

Discussion

In the last ten years, the topic of entrepreneurship has attracted attention worldwide. Multinational Enterprises (MNEs) and investors are now complementing more traditional actors in the field of development. Simultaneously there has been a surge of incubator creation.

No matter the environment, a large majority of startup companies (‘startups’) do not survive longer than two to three years. Incubators are designed to support the startups in the early stages of their lifecycle and can leverage the Small and Medium Enterprise (SME) sector for better economic growth. Incubators on the African continent have only been established in recent years as a tool to enable enterprise creation and eventually reap the benefits in terms of job formation and salary disbursement. Some of the key metrics for the success of an incubator include whether the companies that complete the incubation program have more employees, better yield, and more sales. In academic literature there are varying definitions of what constitutes an incubator. The definition adopted for this paper is that of the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (UNECE), which describes an incubator as a “systematic way to support the establishment and growth of a new company… that provides physical space and a number of services to start-ups, helping them through the earlier stages of their development”. This definition was chosen because it is simultaneously inclusive while identifying the main characteristics of an incubator.

The Global Accelerator Learning Initiative (GALI) regularly publishes its latest findings on the effectiveness of incubators and has specific publications tailored for emerging markets. GALI shows that incubators in emerging countries produce increased revenue, create more jobs, and attract higher equity investments and higher rates of access to debt for startups. Therefore, there is some evidence that suggests that incubators could be a useful tool for job creation.

For the region of Sub-Saharan Africa, the benefits of an incubator program match those of the emerging markets sample. The key contribution according to the startups themselves is access to direct funding, network and skill building.

One key issue for the sector is growth acceleration after the initial incubation phase. While incubators are a useful tool for the first phase of creating a company, there is a gap between that initial push and reaching the stage where impact investors can confidently invest their funds. Existing literature for the startup segment has plenty advice how to deal with the challenge of scaling a nascent business. Unfortunately literature is lacking when it comes to scaling startups in developing countries.

In their previous research the authors have found that the effects of an incubator program go beyond the standard key performance indicators. Notably, the main benefits reported by experts in the field and former participants was the empowerment of the applicants and self-confidence building.

Learning how to think like an entrepreneur can be a powerful instrument to counteract youth unemployment and a negative outlook on the future

Innovative ideas can change whole sectors. Mpesa, a mobile phone-based money transfer and financing company, is one example of a disruptive business idea that has transformed the financial landscape of Kenya. Through such enterprises the concept of mobile money has seen rapid scaling in Sub-Saharan Africa.

Incubator programs offer, prospective entrepreneurs the opportunity to learn how to deal with local market environments and regulatory processes, as well as how the informal business networks are structured. To enable this, it is crucial to have local talent with the required experience to impart this knowledge to the founders. Incubators have the potential to generate substantial effects because the solutions are locally tailored.

Conclusion

Incubators can be a possible solution in the toolkit to counteract youth unemployment. GALI presents promising data from emerging economies and Sub-Saharan Africa. While the early stage incubation programs appear to be effective, there is still a gap when it comes to the next stage of scaling and deal flow.

The potential of incubators goes far beyond the standard key performance indicators such as job creation or equity investment. Rather the most significant benefit is the empowerment of participants.
